Analysing Influencers with Sentiment Analysis

Juan Martinez
September 13, 2024
Updated on September 27, 2024

This analysis was part of the joint article:

Julián Castro-Rea and Juan Martinez, “Unlikely Influencers: Right-Wing Politics Online”. Paper presented to the 2023 Canadian Political Science Association Conference at York University, Toronto.

This article focuses on three right-wing political influencers, arguably the most popular in Canada: Jordan Peterson, Ben Shapiro and Andrew Tate. It will be argued that these three individuals, although very different from each other, are nonetheless jointly contributing in a major way to metapolitics...

A follow-up and discussion of these three men’s activities online over recent time will be offered. A computer-based, qualitative and quantitative measure of their impact is the basis for the analysis; tracking the content of their messaging, their number of followers and readership of their posts, and the impact these posts have on the followers. 

As part of this article, I extracted the post and corresponding comments in X (Twitter), Instagram and YouTube to measure:

  1. The type of "sentiment" by the influencers.
  2. The type of "sentiment" by users in social media.

The purpose was to understand further their approach to social media and how a user in these networks perceived their ideas.

The data was obtained in JSON format. Using a Python script the comments/texts were extracted and transformed to CSV.

For the sentiment analysis it was used is the Twitter-roBERTa-base which is based on around 124 million tweets in English from 2018 to 2021 as an input to train the algorithm (Python Script). This model is hosted and available in the Huggingface community and created by the Research Group in Natural Language Processing at Cardiff University (Huggingface 2023).

After analyzing around 2000 comments the results were visualized in bar charts using ggplot2 library in R. These are some of the plots created.

Based on the analysis and visualizations created, we made the following findings:

  1. Although Andrew Tate's polemic figure, his publications show a more balanced set of emotions in his publications. Similarly is the reaction of the users.
  2. In the cases of Ben Shapiro and Jordan Peterson, they use more negative language when expressing their ideas while receiving similar reactions from the users.
